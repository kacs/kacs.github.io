The December 1999 Puzzler: An Answer for the Curious.

Linus Pauling (From an address given at Yale University, October 1947):

"When it has become possible to determine in detail the molecular structure of the vectors of disease and of the constituents of the cells of the human body, it will be possible to draw up the specifications of a specific chemotherapeutic agent to protect the body against a specific danger, and then to proceed to synthesize the agent according to the specifications."

Our clairvoyant is the inimitable Linus Pauling. There are two perspectives to interpret his statement. The first is that he was reflecting backward, with this quote merely an elegant restatement of Emil Fischer's "lock and key" hypothesis. If one knows the design of the tumblers of the lock, surely one can then intuit the geometry of the key? The second is that he was visionary, with this quote reflecting a forceful statement of his emerging understanding of the chemical basis for biochemical structure, catalysis, and information encoding. Pauling was, I believe, doing both. But the clarity of his vision, in contrast to the muddled thought that still gripped mechanistic and structural biological chemistry, is remarkable. The concept of vitalism as an impenetrable, mystical component of biological phenomena had only recently succumbed to the heroic experimental efforts of Sumner, Northrup and Kunitz. In 1947 the structural aspects of proteins were speculative debate, and the structural basis of genetics a brash new frontier. Yet Pauling knew that it was all chemistry! At the most fundamental level organic, inorganic and biological chemistry were identical: these are disciplines of atoms, in beautiful arrays of molecules. Pauling resonated with absolute certitude that the entirety of the tangible &endash; whether by touch or by experiment &endash; biochemical universe was molecular. And the molecular machines that controlled biological phenomena (whatever their structure) could be deceived by mimicry of the very molecules they manipulated, and that this was indeed the past, and the future, of chemotherapeutic design.
