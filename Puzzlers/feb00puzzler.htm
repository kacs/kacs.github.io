Answer to the February Puzzler

" 'Had silicon been a gas I would have been a major general' - James McNeill Whistler, from the vantage of celebrity on his departure from West Point in 1854, after flunking chemistry."

Cited in "Lab Notes: Experiments in Chemistry and Jazz" by Don Asher, Harper's Magazine, May 1999, p. 61 ff.

This whimsical quote - it sounds like it belongs in a Gilbert & Sullivan operetta - is found as the introductory comment to Don Asher's autobiographical reminiscence of his trials and tribulations in chemistry. Mr. Asher's real calling in life turned out to be as a jazz pianist, and his humorous essay makesit clear why. If anyone would like a photocopy of Asher's essay please contact me. (This was the first of two essays published last year on this topic. The second, a panegyric to chemistry by Oliver Sacks (very different than Asher's essay) appeared late last year in The New Yorker. An expanded version will be published shortly in book form.)

James McNeill Whistler (1834-1903) was born in Massachusetts but spent much of his early youth in Russia where his father, a career military officer, was assigned. Following his father's death in 1849, he returned to the United States to enroll in the West Point Military Academy in 1851. Within three years, however, an unfortunate experience with chemistry led to his dismissal from the Academy. After brief employment with the Coast & Geodesic Service (where he assisted in mapmaking, and received useful training in etching) he left the United States in 1855, never to return. The rest is art history.
