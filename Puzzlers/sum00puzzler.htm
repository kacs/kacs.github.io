Answer to the Summer 2000 Puzzler

The Liebig’s Tube (170th Anniversary! in 2000) and the A.C.S. Logo

The triangular, five&emdash;bulb tube that is surrounded by the "ACS" in the A.C.S. logo is the Liebig’s Tube. For years, this then&emdash;ubiquitous tube was identified with chemistry as well as was any other icon. (This identification no longer exists, as evidenced by the very few responses I received.) What is the Liebig’s tube, and what was its purpose?

In the early part of the 19th century the determination of the empirical formula of a pure compound, by combustion analysis, was a literal hotbed of chemical inquiry. Among the contentious issues of the time &endash; provoking sharp debate &endash; were such questions as isomerism (can two different compounds possess the same empirical formula?) and basicity (is there a direct correspondence between the number of nitrogens in a molecule and the equivalents of acid required for neutralization?). The answers to these questions demanded, above all else, the mutual agreement of the empirical formula. The laboratory that contributed decisively to this conundrum, by the development of a reproducible (precise and accurate) method of combustion analysis, was that of Justus von Liebig (at the University of Giessen). The Liebig’s tube, filled with KOH, was used for the combustion analysis (vide infra: presumably to trap CO2). Although first described in 1830, it was notoriously difficult to make, and it was some time thereafter before it became a common piece of laboratory glassware. A practical method for its fabrication was devised by the master glassblower at Giessen, Carl Ettling. Ettling taught at least one other glassblower how to make the tube, after which it became more available. With the advent of reliable empirical formula data, the issue of isomerism was soon resolved (Liebig did not directly contribute to this debate) as well as the relationship of nitrogen quantity to basicity (Liebig originally believed this was the case, but later corrected his conclusion). Liebig &endash; an inorganic, organic, analytical and agricultural chemist &endash; provides a most fitting image to our logo.

Liebig’s role in the development of modern chemistry is described succinctly by Brock, in a biographical essay (Chem Rev. 1996, 383) and in Brock’s fabulous short history of chemistry ("The Norton History of Chemistry"). If you’re a chemist you should read this book! Regrettably, neither describes the actual use of the tube (although see Brock’s history p. 198 for a fascinating description of the calculation of an empirical formula: nothing like this today.) I presume that the answer might be found in Brock’s full biography of Liebig, that I do not have in my library. If you do &endash; or know the answer &endash; please let me know!
